    Rarely seen, but found in nearly every device we touch, tin is the silent backbone of the modern world. Used in semiconductors and solder, it is essential to electronics and AI technologies. Indonesia is currently the world's second-largest exporter of tin, with nearly 90% of its supply drawn from the Bangka and Belitung Islands. The mining has left a devastating footprint on the region's landscape, having already destroyed 1,000,000 hectares of land, 240,000 hectares of mangrove forests, and 5,000 hectares of coral reefs. Indonesia's state-owned PT Timah is the country's largest single producer, employing around 10,000 workers. However, in 1990, due to a decline in global tin prices and internal restructuring, the company shed roughly 26,000 employees. In the aftermath, small-scale artisanal mining became the dominant form of production, exacerbating unsafe working conditions, environmental destruction, and underreported fatalities. This story offers a glimpse not only into the life of a boy shaped by tin's invisible reach, but also into the often hidden ethical cost of technological consumption.

"You can always tell how the fishing went by the way Ayah eats."

He doesn't eat much.

I overheard him telling Mama he only caught two kilos of tenggiri. "They're getting worse," he sighed, talking about the dredgers. "I don't know what to do. I need to go further out, but I can't afford the gas. Maybe I can hitch a ride with Bayu tomorrow."

"*Jangan khawatir, makanlah,*" Mama whispered. "Don't worry, eat."

My family is from Batu Belubang, in central Bangka. Kakek told me that when he grew up here, the coastal water was a clear tropical blue—you could even see your toes wiggle. There were kembung and cumi-cumi in the open waters, kakap merah in the reefs, and thousands of belanak swimming through the mangroves.

Back then, he said, PT Timah mostly mined on land. The company didn't start moving offshore until after Ayah was born.

I've never seen what they saw.

The mangroves were stripped away before I was even born. Ayah says the tide used to leave tiny fish in the roots. Crabs, too. Now it's just a fog of mud.

But the water still pulls us in. Sometimes, when Ardi and I go swimming, we pass over patches of coral. The water's always cloudy from the dredgers, so it's hard to see. But what we do see looks lifeless and abandoned. We make sure to swim carefully, not to protect it but to protect us.

I never liked our coral. It's dead anyway.

But Ardi didn't agree. "It's not dead," he'd tell me. "It's just hiding. Waiting for the mud to clear."

---

Yesterday, we sat down on the beach after school. Neither of us wanted to swim. I guess it wasn't hot enough.

Ardi told me he was quitting school, it had been his last day. He said his mama, Mak Lani, has been struggling so it's time to start making some money.

"School's pointless. Time to grow up." He said.

He told me Om Ronny was going to let him join the dredge tomorrow, trial run. He's always offering spots to boys who want to work. I like him. He jokes a lot and gives us es jeruk when it's hot. But Ayah always tells me to stay away.

"Lucky," I said. "My family would never let me tin, not after what PT Timah did to Kakek."

Ardi shrugged. "Then I'll make enough for both of us."

"Oh yeah?" I laughed. "We'll see about that."

---

After dinner I walked down to the beach, to where Om Ronny and his crew dock their rafts. You can usually see them from the shore but they were a little further out today.

I was still full from dinner so I lay down under a coconut tree. The sun was strong. No clouds. The feathered leaves stirred gently above me, casting shadows that slipped across my face like a tide brushing along the shore. There one moment, gone the next.

---

"*Cepat! Cepat!*" Hurry! Hurry!

I pushed myself up on my elbows. I saw people rushing towards the water and a crowd was starting to form around Om Ronny's raft. I stood up. Something was off.

I walked towards the crowd. Om Ronny and I locked eyes for a moment. He quickly turned away.

"*Selangnya putus, sialan!*" I heard someone shout. "The damn hose snapped!"

It got quiet after that, not because the yelling stopped but because my ears tuned out. I was scanning the crowd for Ardi. He has to be here somewhere.

I faintly heard his name, maybe twice, I don't remember. Then I heard Mak Lani scream, it pierced through my deaf ears. It was loud again. Commotion.

But I didn't need to hear more. My body already knew.

I felt numb. I had to leave.

I started toward where we used to swim. He told me they'd drop him in ten meters with a hose and let him dredge for a couple hours. He made it sound easy.

The tide was low. The sinking sun had turned the whole world orange.

I could still hear the noise behind me, but it felt far away now.

I stopped and looked out towards the muddy water, as if something out there might still my racing thoughts.

My search was interrupted by a faint tap.

A piece of coral washed up by my feet. Not bright, but not white either, faint red along the edges.

I picked it up and watched the color fade in my hand.

"Ardi was right," I whispered.

They weren't all dead after all.

## Overview

This paper argues that the relationship between artificial intelligence and human agency is zero-sum. Drawing on case studies from aviation, medicine, hiring, and criminal justice, it traces how AI systems transition from assistive tools to primary decision-makers, and examines the cognitive, psychological, and institutional mechanisms that make that shift difficult to reverse.

---

On October 29, 2018, Lion Air Flight 610, a Boeing 737 MAX, crashed into the Java Sea thirteen minutes after takeoff, killing all 189 passengers and crew. Less than five months later, Ethiopian Airlines Flight 302, also a Boeing 737 MAX, went down shortly after departure from Addis Ababa, claiming 157 more lives. The FAA initially resisted grounding the fleet, citing insufficient evidence of a shared cause. Within days, international pressure and emerging technical data forced a reversal.

Investigators identified the Maneuvering Characteristics Augmentation System (MCAS) as central to both crashes. MCAS was a software control system designed to stabilize the aircraft under specific aerodynamic conditions. Acting on faulty sensor input, it repeatedly pushed the nose of each plane downward. Pilots retained nominal authority over the aircraft, but the system's behavior effectively made that authority meaningless. In the moments that counted, control had already shifted.

This failure raises a broader question: how do systems designed to assist human judgment evolve into systems that supplant it?

The Boeing disasters were not anomalous engineering failures, and treating them as such misses what made them representative. The same dynamic: gradual delegation of consequential authority to automated systems, followed by erosion of human capacity to intervene, appears in medicine, hiring, criminal justice, and organizational management. The forces driving this are not purely technological. They include cognitive constraints that make offloading attractive, psychological patterns that normalize dependence, and institutional incentives that reward efficiency without accounting for what gets displaced. In this sense, the relationship is zero-sum: as machines absorb more of the judgment, the human side contracts.

## From Tool to Cognitive Offloading

When AI first enters an environment, the framing is usually augmentation, a tool to assist rather than supplant. And in the short term, this is often accurate. But there's a secondary effect that tends to get overlooked: as AI absorbs more of the cognitive work, people lose practice at the underlying task. Efficiency and skill atrophy can happen at the same time, for the same reason.

Navigation is a useful case. Before GPS became ubiquitous, getting from one place to another required active engagement. Not long ago, this often meant relying on memory or printed MapQuest directions, tracking each turn, mentally registering landmarks, building some internal sense of where you were relative to where you were going. The process was imperfect, but it demanded genuine spatial reasoning. Modern systems have automated all of that. Real-time traffic data, predictive rerouting, dynamic recalculation; users retain control over the destination, but the cognitive work of getting there has been outsourced. What remains is following turn-by-turn prompts.

The pattern shows up in medicine too. Systems like IBM Watson Health and Aidoc parse large datasets to flag patterns, triage cases, and surface diagnostic recommendations faster than any individual clinician could. Physicians still make the formal call, but the analytical work of reaching a diagnosis increasingly happens upstream, inside the algorithm. In practice, this tends to shift the physician's role toward evaluating machine output rather than constructing a diagnosis from scratch.

Researchers describe this as *cognitive outsourcing*: delegating internal mental processes to external systems. Humans have always used tools to extend what they can do; writing, maps, and calculators are obvious predecessors. But AI systems are different in kind. Rather than storing or displaying information, they interpret it, weigh it, and return conclusions, often without any visible intermediate reasoning. The distinction matters. Prior tools extended what humans could do; these systems increasingly do the doing themselves.

John Sweller's Cognitive Load Theory helps explain why this pattern keeps repeating. Working memory has limits, and when tasks grow demanding, people naturally look for ways to offload parts of the work (Sweller, 1988). AI systems are unusually effective at absorbing the hardest components, and given genuine cognitive constraints, people tend to let them. What Sweller's framework doesn't fully capture is what disappears in the process: spatial reasoning, diagnostic intuition, the capacity to work through ambiguity without algorithmic scaffolding. These degrade with disuse and require regular exercise to maintain.

What remains, eventually, is formal authority without the underlying reasoning that would give it substance. Outputs get reviewed and approved rather than genuinely interrogated, which is a different cognitive act than it appears to be from the outside.

## From Assistance to Deference

As AI systems become more embedded in organizational workflows, something subtler happens to how people relate to them. Outputs that were initially understood as recommendations start functioning as verdicts, not because anyone decided this should happen, but because the cognitive cost of consistently second-guessing them in real time is high.

Researchers call this *automation bias*: the tendency to defer to automated systems even when those systems are wrong (Skitka et al., 1999). The effect is most pronounced under complexity, time pressure, and cognitive load (Parasuraman & Manzey, 2010), conditions that describe most high-stakes professional environments. The implication is uncomfortable: the situations where independent verification matters most are also the situations where it's least likely to occur.

Hiring illustrates this concretely. Platforms like Torre.ai automate sourcing, screening, ranking, and evaluation across large candidate pools, essentially every stage before an offer letter. When a recruiter works from a pre-ranked list with algorithmic confidence scores attached, the practical pressure to treat that ranking as settled rather than preliminary is real. What looks like an active decision becomes closer to a sign-off.

The stakes are higher in criminal justice. Tools like COMPAS generate recidivism risk scores used to inform sentencing and parole decisions. Judges, operating under substantial caseload pressure, frequently treat these outputs as authoritative. ProPublica's 2016 investigation found that 45% of Black defendants were misclassified as higher risk compared to 23% of white defendants. When a flawed score carries legal weight, the system's errors don't just go uncorrected; they get formalized.

Autonomous vehicles illustrate this through a different mechanism. Systems from Waymo and Tesla manage lane positioning, speed regulation, and hazard detection reliably enough that drivers disengage from active monitoring, and studies have linked this to measurable reductions in reaction time and situational awareness, particularly in unexpected situations requiring immediate manual response. Full autonomy isn't needed for this dynamic to take hold. It only needs to become easier, socially and cognitively, to follow the system than to override it.

## The Psychological Consequences of AI Dependence

Sustained reliance on AI doesn't just change how people work; it changes how they think, and the changes tend to compound in ways that aren't immediately obvious.

One mechanism is fairly direct: as AI handles the cognitively demanding portions of a task, people exercise those cognitive processes less often, and the relevant skills degrade from disuse. Spatial reasoning, diagnostic intuition, the ability to navigate ambiguity without algorithmic support, and these don't remain available on standby. They require regular practice to maintain, and the less frequently they're engaged, the faster they fade. The pace is slow enough that the decline often isn't noticed until the skill is needed under conditions where it can no longer be relied on.

A second mechanism is what psychologists call learned helplessness, a pattern first identified by Martin Seligman and Steven Maier. When people repeatedly find that their actions make no meaningful difference to outcomes, they tend to stop acting. In AI-assisted environments, the version of this is subtler than it might sound: overrides aren't necessarily blocked, but they're consistently outweighed by systems with greater information-processing capacity and institutional backing. Over time, the perceived weight of one's own judgment starts to diminish. The 737 MAX cockpit is an extreme illustration: the pilots who couldn't counteract the MCAS system hadn't lost confidence in their flying ability, but they had effectively lost the practical capacity to act on it in the time available.

A third factor is adaptation. People adjust quickly to improvements in convenience and, once adjusted, treat the new baseline as normal rather than as an upgrade. Tools that were initially perceived as optional gradually become functionally required, often without a clear moment at which that transition happened. The frame shifts from "this is useful" to "I need this to function," and with that shift, the motivation to maintain independent capability tends to disappear, because the need for it is no longer felt.

Writing illustrates where this leads. Constructing an argument has always been both a communicative act and a cognitive one; the process of drafting is also a process of working through what you actually think. As language models take on more of the drafting, editing, and ideation, that cognitive dimension gets outsourced alongside the output. Users aren't just getting help with expression; in many cases, they're offloading the thinking itself.

Each of these mechanisms reinforces the others, and the cumulative effect is not dramatic. The end state looks like competent, routine performance in an AI-assisted environment, which is part of what makes it worth examining carefully.

## Corporate Incentives: The Structural Drive Toward Automation

The organizational logic driving AI adoption isn't neutral. Companies invest in these systems primarily because they're faster, more scalable, and cheaper than human alternatives, and those objectives align directly with increasing automation, not with preserving human capability alongside it.

A system that screens ten thousand job applications overnight, analyzes radiological images at throughput no single radiologist could match, or generates risk assessments in real time represents a straightforward investment case. Whether such systems preserve the judgment and skills of the people working alongside them is not a question that typically appears in that case. The organizational pressure runs in one direction, and the incentive structure reflects it.

Deployment also compounds over time. Each use cycle generates data that improves model performance, which justifies further reliance, which generates more data. The more embedded a system becomes, the harder it is to make a credible case for a slower, more labor-intensive process built around human judgment instead. That's not a market failure; it's the incentive structure producing the outcome it was built to produce.

What's missing from these calculations is any accounting for what gets displaced. Skill erosion, growing operational dependency, the gradual contraction of what human workers are actually expected to contribute; none of this appears on a balance sheet. There is no internal pressure to preserve human judgment when the system is performing well against the metrics that actually matter organizationally.

## The Snowball Effect: From Adoption to Irreversibility

Once AI systems are integrated into institutional workflows, their influence tends to extend well beyond the original use case. Workflows get rebuilt around model outputs. Performance benchmarks shift toward what algorithms are good at measuring. Teams reorganize around system interfaces rather than the domain expertise the system was originally meant to support. At some point, the AI stops supplementing the existing process and starts constituting it.

Economists describe this kind of dynamic as *path dependence*: current choices constrained by prior commitments in ways that grow harder to reverse over time. Once an organization has invested in AI infrastructure and redesigned its processes around it, the cost of reverting is substantial, and not only technically. New employees are trained to operate within these systems rather than independently of them. Institutional knowledge coalesces around managing the tool rather than performing the task it was meant to assist. The organization becomes structurally dependent in ways it didn't fully anticipate when the system was first adopted.

Legal and regulatory frameworks add another layer. When AI systems are embedded in formal decision-making, sentencing, medical certification, lending, hiring, removing them requires redefining accountability, liability, and operating standards. Displacement stops being a technical decision and becomes an institutional one, requiring coordination across organizations that have each built on the assumption the system will persist.

What started as a productivity tool becomes structural. And dismantling structural components means reconstructing not just the technology but the workflows, expectations, and institutional knowledge built around it, most of which no longer exist in their pre-AI form.

## Conclusion: The Cost of Delegation

The Boeing crashes remain a useful reference point because they offer a rare case of this dynamic being traceable to a specific catastrophic outcome. A system designed to improve stability ended up overriding the pilots' ability to respond, not through a sudden failure, but through accumulated trust in a tool whose limits weren't fully understood until those limits were tested under the worst possible conditions.

In most domains, the same logic operates more diffusely. Medical decisions get shaped by AI-generated recommendations that clinicians don't have time to fully interrogate. Hiring pipelines compress judgment into approval. Risk assessments with documented racial bias inform sentences that affect people's lives. The failures are harder to attribute to any single event, and in many cases are never recognized as failures; they look like normal operations, which is part of what makes them hard to challenge.

Agency isn't only the ability to act. It includes the capacity to understand what you're doing and why, to reason through a problem rather than evaluate what an algorithm concluded. When that capacity is consistently outsourced, the human role changes in something more than degree. The erosion is gradual enough to normalize, and normalization is its own kind of loss.

Whether AI continues to advance isn't really the question; it will. What's worth asking is whether that advancement is the product of deliberate choices about what humans should continue doing, or whether the default trajectory, shaped by cognitive convenience and organizational incentive, gets to make those choices instead. Without deliberate intervention at the level of system design, institutional policy, and individual practice, the outcome isn't collaboration. It's displacement of a kind that looks, from the inside, like progress.

Efficiency is easy to measure. The gradual loss of the sense that one's conclusions reflect one's own reasoning is harder to quantify, and easier to overlook until it's already gone.

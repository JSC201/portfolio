On October 29, 2018, Lion Air Flight 610, a Boeing 737 MAX, crashed into the Java Sea thirteen minutes after takeoff, killing all 189 passengers and crew. Less than five months later, on March 10, 2019, Ethiopian Airlines Flight 302—also a Boeing 737 MAX—crashed shortly after departure, claiming 157 lives. In both cases, initial responses from the Federal Aviation Administration (FAA) emphasized a lack of clear similarity between the incidents, and the aircraft was deemed airworthy. Within days, however, mounting international pressure and emerging technical evidence forced a reversal: the global fleet was grounded.

Subsequent investigations identified the Maneuvering Characteristics Augmentation System (MCAS) as a central factor in both crashes. MCAS, a software-based control system designed to stabilize the aircraft under specific aerodynamic conditions, repeatedly forced the nose of each aircraft downward based on faulty sensor input. Although pilots retained nominal authority, the system's behavior effectively overrode human intervention. In critical moments, control shifted from pilot to machine.

This failure raises a broader question: how do systems designed to assist human decision-making evolve into systems that supersede it?

This paper argues that the Boeing 737 MAX disasters are not anomalous engineering failures, but symptomatic of a broader trajectory in artificial intelligence–driven automation. Across domains including medicine, hiring, and criminal justice, AI systems are transitioning from auxiliary tools to primary decision-makers. This shift is not solely technological; it is reinforced by cognitive limitations, psychological adaptation, and institutional incentives that collectively favor increasing machine autonomy. The central claim is that human agency and artificial intelligence operate within a zero-sum framework: as decision-making authority is transferred to machines, human capacity for independent judgment correspondingly diminishes.

## From Tool to Cognitive Offloading

The earliest stage of artificial intelligence integration is often framed as augmentation: systems designed to assist, rather than replace, human decision-making. In this configuration, AI functions as a cognitive support layer, extending human capability by reducing the computational burden associated with complex tasks. However, this framing obscures a more consequential transformation. As AI systems assume responsibility for information processing, humans do not merely become more efficient—they begin to relinquish the underlying cognitive functions required to perform those tasks independently.

Navigation technologies provide a clear illustration of this shift. Prior to the widespread adoption of AI-driven systems such as Google Maps, route planning required active spatial reasoning, memory, and continuous environmental feedback. Not long ago, getting from one place to another often meant relying on memory or following printed MapQuest directions—paying close attention to each turn, correcting mistakes on the fly, and building an internal sense of where you were in relation to your destination. That process, while imperfect, required constant engagement with the task itself.

Today, modern navigation systems automate these processes entirely, incorporating real-time traffic data, predictive modeling, and dynamic rerouting. While the user retains nominal control over the destination, the cognitive processes required to reach it—path optimization, error correction, and spatial awareness—are fully externalized. The human role is reduced to execution.

A parallel transformation is evident in medicine. AI-driven diagnostic systems such as IBM Watson Health and Aidoc analyze large-scale medical datasets to identify patterns, prioritize cases, and generate diagnostic recommendations. Although physicians retain formal authority over medical decisions, the analytical burden of diagnosis is increasingly transferred to algorithmic systems. In practice, this redefines the physician's role from primary diagnostician to evaluator of machine-generated output.

This redistribution of cognitive labor can be understood through the concept of *cognitive outsourcing*: the delegation of internal mental processes to external systems. While humans have long relied on tools such as writing, maps, and calculators to extend their capabilities, AI-driven systems differ in both scope and function. Rather than merely storing or displaying information, they interpret, prioritize, and generate conclusions, often without transparent intermediate reasoning. The result is not simply assistance, but substitution at the level of cognition itself.

Cognitive Load Theory, proposed by John Sweller, provides a formal framework for understanding why this transition occurs. The theory posits that human working memory has a limited capacity for processing information. When confronted with cognitively complex tasks, individuals naturally seek to minimize mental effort by offloading components of the task to external systems (Sweller, 1988). AI systems, by design, exploit this constraint by absorbing the most cognitively demanding aspects of decision-making.

However, this reduction produces a secondary effect. As reliance on external systems increases, the frequency with which individuals engage in the underlying cognitive processes decreases. Over time, this leads to a gradual erosion of skill—in navigation, individuals become less capable of forming internal spatial maps; in medicine, practitioners risk diminished diagnostic intuition as pattern recognition is increasingly delegated to algorithms.

> At this stage, the human operator retains formal authority over decisions. Yet this authority becomes increasingly superficial. Decision-making is no longer grounded in internally constructed reasoning, but in externally generated outputs. The individual does not cease to decide; rather, they cease to process.

## From Assistance to Deference

As artificial intelligence systems become more deeply embedded in decision-making environments, their role expands beyond cognitive support into functional authority. What begins as assistance gradually evolves into deference, where human operators increasingly rely on system outputs not as inputs to be evaluated, but as conclusions to be accepted.

This behavioral shift is captured by the concept of *automation bias*, first identified by Linda J. Skitka and colleagues, which describes the human tendency to favor recommendations from automated systems even when those recommendations may be incorrect (Skitka et al., 1999). Automation bias is particularly pronounced in environments characterized by complexity, time pressure, and high cognitive load—conditions under which independent verification becomes costly or impractical (Parasuraman & Manzey, 2010).

This dynamic is increasingly visible in hiring practices. Platforms such as Torre.ai deploy AI systems that automate large portions of the recruitment pipeline, including sourcing, screening, ranking, and evaluation of candidates. Recruiters, presented with pre-ranked candidates and algorithmically generated evaluations, may rely on these outputs as authoritative rather than as tools requiring critical scrutiny. The decision is no longer independently constructed; it is endorsed.

A similar pattern emerges in the criminal justice system through the use of risk assessment tools such as COMPAS (Correctional Offender Management Profiling for Alternative Sanctions). These systems generate probabilistic evaluations of recidivism risk to inform judicial decisions, including sentencing and parole. Autonomous driving systems further illustrate this transition. Technologies developed by companies such as Waymo and Tesla are designed to manage complex driving tasks, including lane positioning, speed regulation, and hazard detection. As the system assumes control over routine operations, the driver's level of active engagement declines. Studies have shown that reliance on such systems is associated with delayed reaction times and reduced situational awareness, particularly in moments requiring sudden intervention.

> Across these domains, a consistent pattern emerges. AI systems do not need to fully replace human decision-makers to assume functional authority. It is sufficient that their outputs become the default from which decisions proceed.

## The Psychological Consequences of AI Dependence

The expansion of artificial intelligence within decision-making environments is accompanied by measurable psychological and cognitive shifts that alter how individuals engage with tasks, evaluate information, and exercise judgment. Three compounding mechanisms drive this transformation.

**Cognitive atrophy.** Cognitive Load Theory predicts that as AI absorbs the most demanding aspects of information processing, individuals engage less frequently in these processes independently. The consequence is a form of skill atrophy—tasks that were once cognitively demanding become externally managed, and the internal capabilities required to perform them weaken through disuse.

**Learned helplessness.** First identified by Martin Seligman and Steven Maier, learned helplessness occurs when individuals repeatedly exposed to situations in which their actions have limited effect cease to attempt intervention altogether. In contexts where system outputs are consistently prioritized and attempts to override them are rendered ineffective, individuals begin to internalize the diminished impact of their own judgment. This dynamic was directly implicated in the Boeing 737 MAX crashes, where the inability of pilots to counteract the MCAS system exemplified the consequences of reduced functional control.

**The hedonic treadmill.** The hedonic treadmill describes the tendency for individuals to rapidly adapt to improvements in convenience, resetting their expectations accordingly. Applied to AI systems, this explains how tools initially perceived as enhancements become baseline requirements. The psychological framing shifts from augmentation to necessity, further entrenching reliance established through cognitive outsourcing and behavioral deference.

This pattern is increasingly visible in language itself. Writing has traditionally functioned as both a communicative act and a cognitive process through which individuals structure and refine their thinking. The growing use of large language models to generate, edit, or complete written work alters this process fundamentally. Rather than constructing arguments internally, users may begin to externalize ideation itself—delegating not only expression but formulation to the system.

> These mechanisms form a reinforcing cycle. AI systems reduce the need for cognitive effort, which leads to decreased engagement; decreased engagement fosters dependency; and dependency is normalized through psychological adaptation. The result is not a sudden loss of human agency, but a gradual internalization of its absence.

## Corporate Incentives: The Structural Drive Toward Automation

Corporations do not adopt AI systems as neutral tools of augmentation; they adopt them as instruments of optimization. The primary drivers are efficiency, scalability, and cost reduction—objectives that align directly with increasing automation and, consequently, with the displacement of human decision-making.

The shift from assistance to autonomy is not incidental. It is economically selected. An AI system that can screen thousands of job candidates, analyze medical images at scale, or generate risk assessments in real time offers a clear advantage over human-centered workflows. The question, from an organizational perspective, is not whether such systems preserve human agency, but whether they improve throughput and consistency.

Beyond immediate efficiency, AI systems generate secondary advantages that further entrench their adoption. Each cycle of deployment yields additional data, which in turn enhances model performance, reinforcing reliance on the system. The more a system is used, the more valuable it becomes—and the more difficult it is to justify alternatives that rely on human judgment alone.

Crucially, these incentives operate independently of long-term considerations regarding human capability. The erosion of skill, the development of dependency, and the narrowing of human roles are not accounted for within standard measures of organizational performance. There is no internal mechanism within these systems to preserve or prioritize human agency.

## The Snowball Effect: From Adoption to Irreversibility

The expansion of artificial intelligence within institutional systems does not proceed as a series of isolated upgrades, but as a cumulative process that restructures the environment in which decisions are made. Once AI systems are integrated into operational workflows, their influence extends beyond individual tasks, reshaping the surrounding technical, organizational, and cognitive infrastructure.

At the organizational level, decision-making pipelines are reconfigured to incorporate model outputs, performance metrics are recalibrated around algorithmic benchmarks, and workflows are optimized for system efficiency rather than human discretion. These systems do not simply assist existing processes—they redefine them.

This restructuring produces a form of *path dependence*, in which present decisions are constrained by prior technological commitments. Once an organization invests in AI infrastructure, the cost of reverting to human-centered processes increases substantially. Systems become foundational components of the institution itself. Displacing them would require not only technical replacement, but the reconstruction of the workflows, expertise, and expectations built around them.

At the level of human capital, as AI systems assume responsibility for core analytical functions, the role of human operators shifts from domain expertise to system interaction. Workers are trained to interpret outputs and manage interfaces rather than to perform the underlying tasks independently. New entrants are socialized within AI-mediated environments, learning to operate within systems rather than outside them.

Legal and regulatory frameworks further entrench this dependence. Once embedded in formal decision-making processes, the removal of AI systems is not simply a technical decision, but a legal and institutional one, requiring the redefinition of accountability, liability, and operational standards.

> What begins as a tool introduced to enhance performance evolves into a structural dependency that constrains future choices. The system does not merely assist human decision-making; it reorganizes the conditions under which decisions can be made.

---

## Conclusion: The Cost of Delegation

The Boeing 737 MAX crashes were not merely technical failures; they were manifestations of a deeper structural shift in the relationship between humans and the systems they design. In both incidents, a system intended to assist ultimately overrode human judgment, exposing a critical tension at the core of modern automation. This tension is not confined to aviation. It reflects a broader trajectory in which artificial intelligence systems move from supporting human decision-making to reshaping, and in some cases supplanting, it entirely.

Across domains, this progression follows a consistent pattern. Tasks are first externalized through cognitive offloading, reducing the mental effort required for their execution. This is followed by behavioral deference, as individuals rely on system outputs as default points of authority. Over time, these patterns are internalized, producing measurable cognitive and psychological adaptations that reduce independent reasoning. Finally, institutional incentives and infrastructural dependencies reinforce and scale these changes, embedding AI systems within the fabric of organizational decision-making.

The implications extend beyond individual tasks or industries. Agency is not merely the capacity to act, but the capacity to understand, evaluate, and decide. When these processes are consistently externalized, the role of the human changes in kind, not just in degree. Control is not lost abruptly, but redistributed gradually, until its absence becomes normalized.

The relationship between artificial intelligence and human agency is zero-sum. Gains in system autonomy are accompanied by corresponding reductions in human involvement—not only at the level of action, but at the level of cognition itself. If left unexamined, this trajectory does not culminate in collaboration, but in displacement.

The question is not whether AI will continue to advance, but under what conditions its advancement occurs. Without deliberate intervention—at the level of system design, institutional policy, and individual practice—the default trajectory favors increasing automation at the expense of human agency.

> What is at stake is not efficiency, but authorship. The more we delegate, the less we decide; and the less we decide, the less we remain authors of our own actions.
